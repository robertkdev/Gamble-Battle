## Narrative Pillars

Gamble Battle is a gothic betting arena where money, memory, and monsters all use the same ledger. Every combat is a wager made physical. A player does not merely command a squad; the player stakes a claim, buys allies out of the shop, places them on a ritual board, and risks the bankroll that keeps the party alive.

The lore should support the game systems directly:

- Betting is not UI flavor. A bet is a binding stake written into the battle ledger.
- The shop is not a normal market. It is a rotating contract table where allies, debts, bodies, relics, and futures are priced.
- Gold is not just currency. It is the proof that the House still recognizes your claim.
- Losing money is existential. Running out of money means the player's name falls out of the ledger and the run ends.
- The board is not just a grid. Each tile is a legal seat in the arena, and positioning changes who can claim, protect, tax, or kill.
- Units are not generic heroes. They are debtors, collectors, saints, beasts, tutors, executioners, and contracts with legs.
- Roles, goals, and approaches are the game's readable combat language, so the lore should make them feel like social jobs inside the arena rather than abstract tags.

## World Premise: The Table

The central arena is called the Table: a vast, cracked gaming board built inside an old counting-house cathedral. Its tiles are black stone, ivory bone, tarnished gold, and old contract paper sealed under glass. Around it sit balcony tiers of silent lenders, masked spectators, trait syndicates, and house clerks who record every death before the body hits the floor.

The Table exists because ordinary war became too expensive. Kingdoms, syndicates, cults, scholars, monsters, and ruined noble houses agreed to settle certain conflicts through wagered champions instead of armies. Each battle is small enough to be watched, but large enough to move fortunes. A match can transfer mines, bloodlines, item vaults, family curses, or a single soul.

No one owns the Table completely. The House administers it, but the House is not a monarch or a casino brand. It is a bureaucracy of ledgers, adjudicators, shopkeepers, debt-priests, and rules that have outlived every faction that tried to control them.

## The Player: The Backer

The player is a Backer: someone with enough credit to enter the Table, but not enough power to ignore it. A Backer starts with one ally because the House will not extend trust beyond a single opening contract. Every later unit must be purchased, won, rolled, inherited, or justified by the growing ledger.

A Backer is judged by three questions:

- What are you willing to stake?
- Which debts are you willing to buy?
- Can your board survive the answers other Backers bring?

The Backer does not personally swing a weapon. The Backer reads the field, buys contracts, arranges positions, upgrades allies, and decides how much money can be risked before the next fight. The Backer is part tactician, part gambler, part debtor, and part patron saint of bad odds.

## The Battle Loop In Lore

### Betting

Before combat, the Backer locks in a stake. The stake becomes a visible clause in the round's ledger. A cautious bet keeps the run alive but slows growth. A heavy bet invites collapse, but a win can buy the next shop, the next reroll, the next level, or the missing counterpiece.

In-world, this is called "inking the stake." Once inked, the bet cannot be walked back. The House does not care whether the Backer misread the enemy board.

### The Shop

The ally shop is a contract row operated by House factors. Each offer is a priced opportunity: a person, monster, construct, or spirit whose debt can be bought for a round, a run, or until sold back at a loss.

Rerolling is the Backer paying the House to reshuffle what the ledger is willing to reveal. Trader, Mogul, Cartel, and related economy traits should feel like characters bending this process rather than creating money from nowhere.

### The Board

Each tile is a seat with line-of-sight, adjacency, and legal priority. Placing a unit is the Backer assigning a contract to a seat. Moving one unit by one tile can change who is protected, who is exposed, who gets linked, and who dies first.

The design doc notes that each grid slot can have a queue for who takes over when a unit dies. In lore, this is succession seating: if a champion falls, the next claim tied to that seat can step into the legal space of the dead. This gives the arena a contract-law reason for bench depth, replacement, and cascading fights.

### Combat

Combat is automatic because the contracts are already written. Once the bell rings, the Backer cannot reach into the Table and change the wording. Units act according to role, goal, approach, trait, item, target rule, and position. The drama comes from preparation meeting timing.

The best fights should feel like an experiment whose ingredients were visible before the bell: a protected carry, an assassin angle, a formation-breaking support, a zone caster, a tank who can redirect focus, or a marksman who will shred the front if left alone.

### Payout

After combat, the House reconciles the ledger. Wins return stakes, pay gold, trigger trait rewards, and keep the Backer's name active. Losses burn money. A Backer can survive many wounds, but not insolvency.

## Tone

Gamble Battle should read as occult, transactional, and sharp. The setting can be funny in names and silhouettes, but the world should not become soft parody. A unit named Bonko can still be dangerous. A shop can be absurd and predatory at the same time.

Preferred language:

- ledgers, clauses, writs, stakes, receipts, audits, debts, margins, liens, seals
- cracked stone, dry bone, matte gold, dusty robes, soot, old velvet, ink, chalk, wax
- watched fights, legal violence, bought loyalty, trait covenants, exact-count rituals

Avoid leaning too hard on:

- clean heroic fantasy
- modern casino neon
- generic demon war
- shiny cyberpunk
- joke-only character writing
- lore that hides gameplay clarity

## The House

The House is the referee, bank, shop, prison, and archive of the Table. It records who is eligible to fight, how much a Backer owes, which items can be moved, which deaths count, and which contracts can be sold.

The House is powerful because everyone needs it and everyone hates it. A Backer who defeats an enemy board is not only winning a battle. They are forcing the House to update reality in their favor.

House offices worth using in future writing:

| Office | Lore job | Gameplay surface |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Factors | Price and present shop contracts. | Shop offers and rerolls. |
| Auditors | Verify deaths, payouts, and illegal clauses. | Combat results, rewards, and run loss. |
| Croupier-priests | Ink bets and read round stakes. | Bet locking and planning phase. |
| Bailiffs | Enforce tile claims and bench limits. | Board placement and max team size. |
| Appraisers | Judge item value and Catalyst evolution. | Items and completed-item scaling. |
| Tabulators | Track trait thresholds and exact-count clauses. | Trait tracker and synergies. |

## Roles As Arena Jobs

Roles should remain readable in lore because they are how players learn broad expectations.

| Role | Lore job |
| --- | --- |
| Tank | Own the first contact and make the enemy waste force. |
| Brawler | Stay in the ugly middle long enough that the enemy runs out of clean answers. |
| Assassin | Bypass the polite fight and force the backline to pay. |
| Marksman | Turn protected distance into reliable punishment. |
| Mage | Change timing, space, or damage shape through casts. |
| Support | Make another plan unfair, then dare the enemy to find the source. |

## Goals And Approaches In Lore

Primary goals are the legal win condition written on a unit's contract. Approaches are the tools the unit is allowed to use to reach that condition.

For example:

- A tank with `frontline_absorb` is contracted to waste enemy focus.
- A support with `peel_carry` is contracted to keep the chosen damage source alive.
- An assassin with `cleanup_execution` is contracted to wait until a death can become a chain.
- A mage with `area_denial_zone` is contracted to make certain tiles expensive.

This lets lore stay specific without bloating the identity catalog. The lore can say "Volt traps one debtor in a blue legal current" while the design layer keeps the broad tags `burst`, `lockdown`, and `dot`.

## Item Lore

Items are collateral. A component is a useful fragment of debt. A completed item is a clause with teeth. Catalyst units can evolve items because they treat collateral as alive: if an object survives enough combats, it has earned the right to become stranger.

Item writing should focus on what the object asks from its holder:

- Does it reward being hit?
- Does it reward crits, casting, survival, or range?
- Does it turn shielding into a detonation?
- Does it convert damage into healing?
- Does it decay, stack, or wait?

Items should feel like deals, not equipment lists.

## Creature And Enemy Lore

Creeps should not feel like disposable blobs. The current Creep concept is a hidden/enemy-only resource in code but a planned unit in the design direction: an Exile/Executioner Assassin with a smooth alien face, hollow eyes, and a tendril spin that hunts low-health targets.

In lore, creeps are failed or unsigned contracts that still learned how to move on the Table. They have no stable patron, which makes them useful as early combat pressure and dangerous as future playable material. The House calls them "unbacked liabilities." Everyone else calls them creeps because that is easier to say before one reaches the backline.

## Faction Tensions

Use these tensions to write events, bios, campaign framing, or future run flavor:

- The House wants every fight to be legible, taxable, and repeatable.
- Cartel and Mogul units want to profit from the House without being owned by it.
- Trader units want options more than loyalty.
- Exile units want exact-count freedom and hate being diluted into normal armies.
- Scholar and Mentor units believe the Table can be solved.
- Chronomancer units believe the Table can be delayed.
- Executioner units believe the Table only matters at the moment a contract ends.
- Catalyst units believe no item is finished just because the House says it is.
- Harmony and Kaleidoscope disagree over wide boards: Harmony wants disciplined restraint, while Kaleidoscope wants maximum active color.
- Liaison units treat difference as power; vertical trait loyalists often treat it as contamination.

## Campaign Frame

If Gamble Battle grows a campaign wrapper, the simplest frame is a climb through increasingly specific debts.

1. Opening fights teach that one ally and one stake can keep a Backer alive.
2. Early shop rounds teach that buying power without preserving money creates future losses.
3. Mid-game fights introduce trait covenants as factions trying to pull the Backer into their style of risk.
4. Later chapters require counter-boards: the Backer must read the enemy's goal and answer with positioning, role mix, or a specific unit.
5. Boss or final-round fights should feel like legal trials with monsters: each boss is a clause the Backer failed to notice earlier.

The campaign does not need a heroic prophecy. The stronger frame is survival through increasingly predatory contracts.

## Writing Rules For Future Lore

1. Tie every lore beat to a visible gameplay behavior.
2. Keep names and hooks compatible with the current identity vocabulary.
3. Do not invent live-state facts for units that are only planned.
4. Let dark comedy come from the contradiction between silly names and serious consequences.
5. Keep the House present but not overexplained.
6. Use money language when describing stakes, shop actions, payout, and risk.
7. Use contract language when describing abilities, traits, roles, and death.
8. Use board language when describing positioning, target access, zone, peel, redirect, and formation-breaking.
9. Preserve Sari's current traitless status until gameplay data assigns traits.
10. Preserve planned units as planning-only until their `.tres` resources and abilities exist.
